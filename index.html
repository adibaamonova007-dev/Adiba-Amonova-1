The Impact of Urban Green Spaces

Urban green spaces — parks, gardens, tree-lined streets, and other vegetated areas — play a critical role in the health and well-being of city residents. As urbanisation continues to accelerate globally, the value of these areas has become increasingly recognised by urban planners and public health researchers alike.

Studies have consistently shown that access to green spaces is associated with lower levels of stress, improved mental health, and greater physical activity. A 2019 study published in Scientific Reports found that people who spent at least two hours per week in natural environments reported significantly higher levels of health and well-being compared to those who did not.

Beyond individual health benefits, urban greenery provides what scientists call "ecosystem services" — benefits that healthy ecosystems provide to humans. These include air purification through the absorption of pollutants, temperature regulation via the "urban heat island" effect, flood mitigation through better water absorption, and the promotion of biodiversity.

Despite these benefits, green spaces in cities face numerous threats. Rapid urban development, rising land values, and budget constraints often result in the reduction or degradation of existing parks and green corridors. This is particularly acute in lower-income neighbourhoods, where residents may have significantly less access to quality green spaces.

Cities such as Singapore and Vienna have become global models for integrating nature into urban design, demonstrating that densely populated cities can prioritise greenery without sacrificing economic development.

1. Minimum weekly time in nature linked to improved well-being?

2. "Ecosystem services" include which of the following?

3. Which cities are cited as models for integrating nature?

4. Which groups have least access to green spaces?
